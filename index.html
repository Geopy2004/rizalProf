Biography

José Rizal was a Filipino nationalist, novelist, and key member of the Filipino Propaganda Movement which advocated political reforms for the colony under Spain. He was executed by the Spanish colonial government for rebellion, and is considered a national hero of the Philippines.

1861

Birth and Early Childhood

Born on June 19 in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines, the seventh child of Francisco Rizal Mercado and Teodora Alonso Realonda. He was baptized three days later by Father Rufino Collantes at the Catholic church in Calamba.

1872

Education at Ateneo

Entered the Ateneo Municipal de Manila (now Ateneo de Manila University), where he excelled in academics, winning numerous medals and prizes. He graduated with a Bachelor of Arts degree with highest honors.

1877-1882

University of Santo Tomas

Enrolled in Philosophy and Letters at the University of Santo Tomas before shifting to Medicine. He later decided to continue his medical studies in Spain due to discrimination against Filipino students at UST.

1882-1887

Studies in Europe

Traveled to Spain to study medicine and philosophy at the Universidad Central de Madrid. Also studied in France and Germany, earning a degree in Ophthalmology. During this time, he became involved in the Propaganda Movement.

1887

Noli Me Tangere Published

Published his first novel, "Noli Me Tangere" (Touch Me Not) in Berlin. The novel exposed the abuses of the Spanish colonial government and the Catholic Church in the Philippines, making Rizal a marked man by Spanish authorities.

1891

El Filibusterismo Published

Published his second novel, "El Filibusterismo" (The Reign of Greed) in Ghent, Belgium. This darker and more political work presented a more revolutionary approach to addressing colonial oppression.

1892

La Liga Filipina and Exile

Founded La Liga Filipina, a civic organization calling for peaceful reforms. Shortly after, he was arrested and exiled to Dapitan in Mindanao for four years, where he practiced medicine, established a school, and engaged in community projects.

1896

Execution and Martyrdom

Arrested, tried, and executed by firing squad on December 30 in Bagumbayan (now Rizal Park), Manila, at age 35. His execution fueled the Philippine Revolution against Spanish rule. His final poem, "Mi Ultimo Adios," was hidden in an alcohol lamp and given to his family.

Literary Works

Rizal's literary works played a crucial role in awakening Filipino nationalism. His writings exposed the abuses of the Spanish colonial government and clergy while advocating for peaceful reforms and education.

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

A scathing indictment of Spanish colonial rule and the Catholic Church's abuses in the Philippines. The novel follows the story of Crisostomo Ibarra and his love for Maria Clara against the backdrop of a corrupt society.

El Filibusterismo (1891)

The sequel to Noli Me Tangere, presenting a more revolutionary approach through the character of Simoun, a wealthy jeweler plotting to overthrow the corrupt system through violent means.

Mi Último Adiós (1896)

Written on the eve of his execution, this 14-stanza poem expresses his love for his country and his acceptance of death. Originally untitled, it was later given the title "Mi Ultimo Pensamiento" by his sister.

To the Filipino Youth (1879)

Written when Rizal was just 18, this poem won first prize in a literary contest. It encourages the Filipino youth to reach their potential and serve their country, calling them the "fair hope of the fatherland."

Travels Abroad

Rizal's travels across Europe, Asia, and America significantly influenced his ideas and writings. He visited numerous countries, studying their cultures, politics, and societies while advocating for Philippine reforms.

Spain (1882-1885)

  • Studied medicine at Universidad Central de Madrid
  • Joined the Propaganda Movement
  • Wrote articles for La Solidaridad
  • Earned degree in Medicine and Philosophy

France (1885-1886)

  • Worked as an assistant to Dr. Louis de Weckert
  • Studied ophthalmology
  • Completed part of Noli Me Tangere
  • Attended lectures at universities in Paris

Germany (1886-1887)

  • Worked at the University Eye Hospital in Heidelberg
  • Published Noli Me Tangere in Berlin
  • Became member of Berlin Ethnological Society
  • Studied German culture and language

Asia (1888-1892)

  • Visited Japan, Hong Kong, and Macau
  • Studied Chinese and Japanese cultures
  • Practiced medicine in Hong Kong
  • Began writing El Filibusterismo

Legacy

Rizal's martyrdom and intellectual contributions made him the symbol of Philippine nationalism. His ideals continue to inspire Filipinos and people around the world fighting for justice and freedom.

National Hero

Officially declared the national hero of the Philippines through Republic Act No. 1425 (Rizal Law) for his peaceful reform advocacy and martyrdom.

Learn More

Rizal Day

December 30, the anniversary of his execution, is commemorated as Rizal Day, a national holiday in the Philippines marked by ceremonies at Rizal Park.

Learn More

Rizal Law

Republic Act 1425 mandates the study of Rizal's life and works in all Philippine schools to instill patriotism and national identity among students.

Learn More

International Recognition

Recognized worldwide as a symbol of peaceful reform. Monuments honoring Rizal exist in Spain, Germany, USA, China, Japan, and other countries.

Learn More

Educational Institutions

Numerous schools, universities, and scholarships bear his name, including the prestigious Rizal High School and Rizal Technological University.

Learn More

Currency and Stamps

Featured on Philippine peso bills and coins, as well as postage stamps, cementing his status as the foremost national hero.
